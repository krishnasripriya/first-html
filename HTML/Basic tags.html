MOTHER TERESA:

Mother Teresa, officially known as Saint Teresa of Calcutta, was a Roman Catholic nun and missionary who devoted her life to serving the poor and sick. She was born on August 26, 1910, in Skopje, which is now part of North Macedonia, and her birth name was AnjezÃ« Gonxhe Bojaxhiu. Her work with the poor in Calcutta, India, made her one of the most famous humanitarians of the 20th century.

In 1946, Mother Teresa experienced what she described as a "call within a call," where she felt compelled by a "voice" to leave the convent and work with the poor. She received permission from the Vatican and founded the Missionaries of Charity in 1950. The primary objective of this congregation was to care for, in her own words, "the hungry, the naked, the homeless, the crippled, the blind, the lepers, all those people who feel unwanted, unloved, uncared for throughout society, people that have become a burden to the society and are shunned by everyone."

Work with the Poor:

Mother Teresa and her fellow sisters began by gathering children from the streets, trending tending to the sick and dying in the slums, and providing basic education. Over the years, the Missionaries of Charity grew into an international organization with thousands of members operating in more than 100 countries.

Their work expanded to include orphanages, schools, hospitals, and hospices for those with terminal illnesses such as HIV/AIDS, leprosy, and tuberculosis. Mother Teresa's dedication to the "poorest of the poor" garnered worldwide attention, and she became a symbol of selfless humanitarianism.
